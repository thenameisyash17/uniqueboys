Communication Systems Q&A

1. Explain Shannon's Channel Capacity Theorem.

Shannon's Channel Capacity Theorem states that the maximum rate at which information can be transmitted over a communication channel with arbitrarily small error probability is given by:

C = B log₂(1 + SNR)

Where:
• C = Channel capacity (bits/second)
• B = Bandwidth of the channel (Hz)
• SNR = Signal-to-Noise Ratio (unitless)

This theorem establishes the fundamental limits of communication systems and shows that bandwidth and SNR can be traded off against each other.

2. Compare AM and FM modulation techniques.

AM (Amplitude Modulation):
• Information is encoded in the amplitude of the carrier wave
• Bandwidth: 2 × message bandwidth
• Susceptible to noise and interference
• Simple circuitry, low cost
• Used in AM radio broadcasting (535-1605 kHz)

FM (Frequency Modulation):
• Information is encoded in the frequency of the carrier wave
• Bandwidth: 2 × (Δf + fₘ) where Δf is frequency deviation
• More resistant to noise (capture effect)
• Requires more complex circuitry
• Used in FM radio broadcasting (88-108 MHz), TV audio

FM generally provides better audio quality and noise immunity compared to AM.

3. What is the Nyquist Sampling Theorem?

The Nyquist Sampling Theorem states that to accurately reconstruct a continuous-time signal from its samples, the sampling frequency (fₛ) must be at least twice the highest frequency component (fₘ) present in the signal:

fₛ ≥ 2fₘ

Where fₘ is the maximum frequency in the signal (also called the Nyquist frequency).

Key Points:
• If fₛ < 2fₘ, aliasing occurs (different signals become indistinguishable)
• The minimum sampling rate (2fₘ) is called the Nyquist rate
• In practice, sampling is done at 2.2 to 2.5 times fₘ to account for practical filter limitations
• This theorem is fundamental to digital signal processing and digital communication systems

4. Explain Pulse Code Modulation (PCM) with block diagram.

Pulse Code Modulation (PCM) is a digital modulation technique where an analog signal is converted into digital form through three main processes:

1. Sampling: The analog signal is sampled at regular intervals (Nyquist rate or higher)

2. Quantization: Each sample amplitude is approximated to the nearest value from a finite set of levels

3. Encoding: Each quantized level is represented by a binary code (usually 8-bit or 16-bit)

PCM Block Diagram:
Analog Input → Low-pass Filter (anti-aliasing) → Sampler → Quantizer → Encoder → Digital Output

Advantages: Noise immunity, regeneration possible, easy multiplexing, secure transmission
Disadvantages: Requires large bandwidth, complex circuitry, encoding/decoding delay

5. What are the different types of noise in communication systems?

Noise in communication systems can be classified as:

1. Internal Noise:
• Thermal Noise (Johnson Noise): Due to thermal agitation of electrons in conductors
• Shot Noise: Due to discrete nature of current flow in semiconductors
• Flicker Noise (1/f Noise): Inversely proportional to frequency, dominant at low frequencies
• Partition Noise: In devices where current divides between multiple paths

2. External Noise:
• Atmospheric Noise: From lightning discharges, dominant at frequencies below 30 MHz
• Extraterrestrial Noise: Solar noise, cosmic noise from stars and galaxies
• Industrial Noise: From electrical machinery, ignition systems, switching equipment
• Man-made Noise: From other communication systems, electronic devices

Noise sets the fundamental limit on communication system performance and is quantified using Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).

6. Compare ASK, FSK, and PSK digital modulation techniques.

ASK (Amplitude Shift Keying):
• Information encoded in the amplitude of the carrier
• Simplest to implement but most susceptible to noise
• Bandwidth efficient but power inefficient
• Used in optical fiber communications, RFID tags

FSK (Frequency Shift Keying):
• Information encoded in the frequency of the carrier
• More resistant to amplitude noise than ASK
• Requires more bandwidth than ASK
• Used in low-speed modems, paging systems, wireless keyboards

PSK (Phase Shift Keying):
• Information encoded in the phase of the carrier
• Most resistant to noise among the three
• Requires coherent detection (carrier recovery)
• Used in high-speed modems, Wi-Fi, satellite communications

Performance order (best to worst): PSK > FSK > ASK

SNR(dB) = 10 log₁₀(Pₛ/Pₙ)

where Pₛ = signal power, Pₙ = noise power
