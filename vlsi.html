VLSI Q&A Bank

1. What is the significance of Moore's Law in VLSI?

Moore's Law predicts that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles approximately every two years, while the cost is halved. This has driven the exponential growth in computing power and miniaturization of electronic devices. In VLSI design, this law has motivated continuous innovation in fabrication processes, design methodologies, and materials to keep up with scaling demands.

2. Explain the CMOS fabrication process.

The CMOS fabrication process involves several key steps: 1) Silicon wafer preparation, 2) Oxidation to create SiO2 layer, 3) Photolithography to define patterns, 4) Etching to remove unwanted material, 5) Ion implantation for doping, 6) Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD) for layer deposition, 7) Metallization for interconnects, and 8) Packaging. The process creates both NMOS and PMOS transistors on the same substrate.

3. What are the advantages of CMOS technology?

CMOS technology offers several advantages: 1) Low static power consumption (only dissipates power during switching), 2) High noise immunity, 3) Wide voltage supply range, 4) Good scalability to smaller dimensions, 5) High packing density, 6) Compatibility with digital and analog circuits, and 7) Relatively simple manufacturing process compared to bipolar technology.

4. Differentiate between combinational and sequential circuits.

Combinational circuits: Output depends only on current inputs, no memory elements, examples include adders, multiplexers, and decoders. Sequential circuits: Output depends on current inputs and previous state (memory), contain memory elements like flip-flops, examples include counters, registers, and finite state machines. Sequential circuits are essential for building memory and state-dependent logic.

5. What is the VLSI design flow?

The VLSI design flow typically includes: 1) Specification and architecture design, 2) RTL (Register Transfer Level) design and coding, 3) Functional verification, 4) Logic synthesis, 5) Gate-level simulation, 6) Physical design (floorplanning, placement, routing), 7) Timing analysis and closure, 8) Design rule checking (DRC), 9) Layout vs. Schematic (LVS) verification, and 10) Tape-out for fabrication.

6. Explain clock skew and jitter in VLSI.

Clock skew: The difference in arrival times of the clock signal at different sequential elements in the circuit. It can cause timing violations if not properly managed. Clock jitter: The deviation of clock edges from their ideal positions in time. Jitter can be random or deterministic and affects the maximum operating frequency. Both skew and jitter are critical considerations in high-speed VLSI design to ensure proper synchronization.
