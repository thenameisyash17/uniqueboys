Fundamental Laws

The Four Laws of Thermodynamics

Understanding the fundamental principles that govern energy transfer and transformation in physical systems

0

Zeroth Law

If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This establishes temperature as a fundamental property.

If A = B and B = C, then A = C

Key Applications:

  • Temperature measurement principles
  • Thermometer calibration
  • Establishing thermal equilibrium
I

First Law

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another. The total energy of an isolated system remains constant.

ΔU = Q - W

Key Applications:

  • Heat engine analysis
  • Energy balance calculations
  • Conservation of energy in systems
II

Second Law

The total entropy of an isolated system can never decrease over time. Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder to a hotter body.

ΔSuniverse ≥ 0

Key Applications:

  • Heat engine efficiency limits
  • Refrigeration cycles
  • Irreversibility in processes
III

Third Law

As temperature approaches absolute zero, the entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero. Absolute zero cannot be reached in a finite number of steps.

S → 0 as T → 0 K

Key Applications:

  • Cryogenics and low-temperature physics
  • Absolute entropy calculations
  • Superconductivity studies

Essential Thermodynamic Equations

Complete collection of formulas, equations, and relationships for thermodynamics

First Law Equations

ΔU = Q - W

ΔU = Change in internal energy

Q = Heat transfer to system

W = Work done by system

h = u + Pv

h = Enthalpy

u = Specific internal energy

P = Pressure

v = Specific volume

Second Law Equations

ΔS ≥ ∫(δQ/T)

ΔS = Entropy change

Q = Heat transfer

T = Absolute temperature

ηth = 1 - (QL/QH)

ηth = Thermal efficiency

QL = Heat rejected

QH = Heat added

Ideal Gas Relations

PV = mRT

P = Pressure

V = Volume

m = Mass

R = Specific gas constant

T = Temperature

Δu = cvΔT

cv = Specific heat at constant volume

cp = Specific heat at constant pressure

cp - cv = R

Process Equations

Isothermal: PV = constant
Adiabatic: PVγ = constant
Isobaric: V/T = constant
Isochoric: P/T = constant

γ = cp/cv (adiabatic index)

Work in isothermal expansion: W = P1V1 ln(V2/V1)
